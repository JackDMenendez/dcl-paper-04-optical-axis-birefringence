# Referee Report — Paper IV, v0.1-DRAFT

**Paper:** *Optical-Axis Anisotropy on the A=1 Discrete Causal Lattice: Kinematic Dispersion and the Gauge-Sector Birefringence Cancellation* (J. Menendez, dated 2026-07-18; Paper IV of the A=1 DCL series, subsuming the former Paper VIII companion study; Paper III deferred).

**Reviewer's one-line verdict:** The algebra is correct (independently verified below, every check passed), the audit discipline is exemplary, and the adjugate mechanism is genuinely elegant — but the paper's own honesty standard, applied one notch harder, changes the status of the kinematic channel: taken at face value the single-tick spinor predicts *O(1)* matter-sector anisotropies (an exact axial flat band, and an amplitude-filtering effect that accumulates linearly in tick count), not just Planck-suppressed dimension-six ones. Recommendation: **major revision before v1.0** — in the friendly sense; the revisions are mostly about scope statements, statuses, and citations, not about wrong equations.

## 1. Summary of the submission

The hop set **V**₁=(1,1,1), **V**₂=(1,−1,−1), **V**₃=(−1,1,−1) uses three of the four cube body-diagonals; the omitted fourth, n̂\* = (1,1,−1)/√3, breaks O_h → D_3d and fixes an optical axis with no new postulate. The paper works this single geometric fact to closed form in two sectors. Kinematic (Sec. 2): the single-tick spinor transfer operator's structure factor is exactly flat along n̂\* and curved across it, M_eff = (4/3)P_⊥, giving a massive-sector group speed (2/3) sin ω |k| sin α, with careful separation of eigenphase magnitude (exact) from group-velocity reading (earned for ω ≠ 0 only; the massless eigenphase is exactly flat, Prop. 2.2). Gauge (Sec. 3): the plaquette geometry supplies ε (eig {1,4,4}) and μ⁻¹ (eig {4,4,16}) bound by μ⁻¹ = γ adj(ε) (Thm. 3.2, any hop vectors), which forces a doubled transverse Fresnel root (Thm. 3.4): vacuum birefringence cancels, conditional on the geometric blocks; what survives is a factor-~2 common-mode speed anisotropy, reported honestly as an O(1), dimension-four Lorentz violation excluded by isotropy experiments for the single-domain construction (FAIL as constructed), with a structural resolution (four-orientation / O_h-restoring hop topology) left open. Section 4 maps falsifiability per channel.

## 3. Overall assessment

The audit table with PASS/PART/FAIL statuses, the explicit "conditional on the geometric blocks" qualifier, the refusal to call the massless spinor a photon, the postdiction-vs-prediction guardrails of Sec. 4.1, and the plainly stated FAIL row are all rare and valuable discipline. The decision to merge the former Paper VIII into this paper was right: the shared axis is the natural unit of exposition. The single most attractive scientific fact in the paper — that the lattice's plaquette geometry lands *automatically* on the unique constitutive relation that kills birefringence — is real, verified, and deserves to be foregrounded even more than it is (see M4 for both the credit it must share and the sharper slogan it earns).

The major points are ordered by how much I think they matter.

## 4. Major points

### M1. The kinematic dispersion is ⊥-only *exactly*, and this makes the matter-sector anisotropy O(1), not dimension-six

The three difference vectors **V**₁−**V**₂ = (0,2,2), **V**₁−**V**₃ = (2,0,2), **V**₂−**V**₃ = (2,−2,0) are each orthogonal to n̂\* — equivalently, all k_∥-dependence of H̃ sits in a global phase e^{i k·V̄} (V̄ = (**V**₁+**V**₂+**V**₃)/3 ∝ n̂\*) that the modulus kills. So |H̃|, and with it both eigenphases *and* both eigenvalue magnitudes, depend on k_⊥ **exactly, to all orders** — not merely at O(k²) as Eq. (4)'s presentation suggests. The paper knows the axial direction is flat ("frozen," Prop. 2.1) but treats the observable content as the dimension-six fractional energy anisotropy (Sec. 4.3). Taken at face value, the operator actually predicts three much stronger things:

**(i) Axial transport is identically zero.** θ(k) = θ(k_⊥) exactly, so ∂θ/∂k_∥ ≡ 0 at every k, not just small k: a wavepacket with momentum along n̂\* does not move, and since ẋ_∥ = ∂θ/∂k_∥ in the Heisenberg sense, x_∥ never moves even under forces that change k_∥. (I checked that the Berry-connection contribution vanishes for this two-component form, so there is no anomalous-velocity escape.) This is an exact flat band along the axis, with the usual flat-band pathology of infinite degeneracy. A hydrogen-like bound state built on this kinetic term is dynamically two-dimensional — the electron cannot orbit in x_∥.

**(ii) The directional anisotropy of the kinetic sector is O(1), not (ka)².** At fixed |k| the kinetic energy is (sin ω/3)|k|² sin²α — the *entire* kinetic term is modulated by sin²α. There is no isotropic kinetic term for the anisotropy to be a small correction *to*. Sec. 4.3's "fractional effect ∼ (Ea/ħc)²" is the right number for the energy anisotropy relative to the *rest phase*, but the quantity a Hughes–Drever-class experiment compares — kinetic/level structure versus orientation — is anisotropic at order unity. If the spinor is any lab particle, this is excluded at zeroth order, no precision required.

**(iii) The kinetic scale itself cannot match a continuum particle.** The lattice ⊥-kinetic coefficient over the continuum k²/2m (same mass, m = sin(ω/2)/a) is (4/3) sin²(ω/2) cos(ω/2): ≈ (4/3)(ma)² ~ 10⁻⁴⁵ for an electron at Planckian a, and ≤ 0.513 at *any* ω (numerically verified). So the single-tick spinor cannot be identified with lab matter's kinematics under any tuning: axially inert, O(1) anisotropic in velocity, kinetically (ma)²-suppressed.

The consequence is not that the derivation is wrong (it isn't) but that the kinematic channel needs the same "as-constructed vs. full-theory" split the gauge channel already gets. Either physical matter is emergent with a different dispersion — in which case the sidereal massive-dispersion channel of Sec. 4.3 cannot be connected to any lab or astrophysical particle until the emergence map is exhibited, and the channel's status should say so — or the spinor is matter and the construction is excluded as it stands, in which case the audit table needs a row parallel to the gauge-sector FAIL. Note the structural cause is the same "three of four" fact yet again: *any* three-hop set has difference vectors spanning a plane, and the frozen axis is that plane's normal. The abstract's "one geometric fact organizes two distinct anisotropies" is really organizing three, and the third (the flat band) is the most dangerous for the matter sector.

### M2. The ω = 0 filtering channel accumulates linearly in tick count; "testability open" looks too sanguine — back-of-envelope, it is excluded at Planckian a

The paper correctly identifies the ω-independent magnitude anisotropy as a filtering effect that "survives into observable amplitudes" (Sec. 2.3, exp_01). But it never puts a number on it, and the number is alarming. The relative damping rate between a transverse and an axial k-component of the same state is per tick ≈ cos²(ω/2)·(4/3)(k_⊥a)²; multiplying by the tick rate c/a gives a per-second rate (4/3)k_⊥² a c — **linear in a**, because the dimension-six per-tick suppression is paid back by the a⁻¹ tick count. The dispersion channels do not enjoy this accumulation (a velocity shift is (ka)² forever); the amplitude channel does. With a = ℓ_P:

- electron at hydrogenic momentum (k ~ 1/a₀): e-folding time ≈ 4×10⁵ s ≈ **5 days**;
- thermal N₂ molecule (k ≈ 2.3×10¹¹ m⁻¹): e-fold ≈ **49 minutes**;
- cold neutron beam (1000 m/s): ≈ **7 days**.

To push the thermal-molecule e-fold past the age of the universe requires a ≲ 7×10⁻¹⁵ ℓ_P. What is *observably* excluded needs modeling care — in a gas, collisions rethermalize on ns timescales, leaving a steady-state momentum-distribution anisotropy of order (filter rate)/(collision rate) ~ 10⁻¹³; for bound states the effect appears as an anisotropic anti-Hermitian term whose orientation-dependence for L ≠ 0 states gives sidereally modulated level widths/T₂ — co-magnetometer territory, with sensitivities around 10⁻³³ GeV against a raw rate scale here of 10⁻²¹–10⁻¹⁹ GeV; matter interferometers with seconds of coherence would see ~10⁻⁶-level anisotropic amplitude asymmetries. Even granting two or three orders of modeling slack, the starting point sits tens of orders above bounds.

Recommendation: quantify this channel to the same standard Sec. 4.3 applies to the dispersion channel, before v1.0. Candidate tamings worth naming in the paper: a minimum-probability floor δp_min that halts filtering at small relative amplitude (this connects directly to the program's standing δp_min investigation); a decoherence/rethermalization analysis showing the anisotropy never integrates; or the same emergence escape as M1 — but then the "real observable, testability open (PART)" row becomes another as-constructed FAIL and should say so. M1 and M2 are the phase and the magnitude of the same eigenvalue λ(k); a joint honest treatment would be natural and would strengthen the paper's central claim to candor.

### M3. The gauge-sector tension: the honesty is right, but the "known structural resolution" is costlier than stated, and it back-reacts on the matter-sector sidereal claim

Remark 3.5 correctly insists the four-domain average is "a fictitious ensemble [the lattice] does not realize." Sec. 4.4's proposed four-orientation vacuum must therefore *not* be an ensemble: a classical mixture leaves every realized branch with its excluded O(1) anisotropy. The fix requires a *coherent, symmetrized* vacuum — orientation promoted to a quantum degree of freedom with a symmetric ground state and tunneling between orientations (an ammonia-inversion analog at the level of vacuum geometry). That is dynamical hop-set geometry: a major extension of the formalism, not a rewiring. Worse, the desired asymmetry of readings — "the induced gauge loop sees ⟨ε⟩ ∝ 𝕀 while a matter excitation retains its dimension-six axis" — needs a mechanism: matter couples to photons, and orientation-definite matter decoheres the vacuum superposition, collapsing the photon's medium into a single (excluded) branch. Whatever decouples the sectors must be exhibited, not assumed; as stated, the escape hatch has the same logical shape as the fallacy Remark 3.5 warns against.

Two further recommendations here. First, cite Collins, Perez, Sudarsky, Urrutia & Vucetich, *PRL* **93**, 191301 (2004): the common-mode tension is a tree-level instance of their well-known result that discreteness generically produces O(1) Lorentz violation unless protected, and engaging that literature explicitly will preempt any referee who suspects the author doesn't know it. (The paper's "recognized naturalness difficulty of emergent-spacetime constructions," p. 23, is exactly this and currently cites nothing.) Second, consider whether the title/abstract should foreground the exclusion. As constructed, single-domain A=1 induced electromagnetism is *refuted* — arguably the paper's most consequential result — and it is presently packaged as a subordinate clause of the cancellation result. A no-go framing ("the birefringence null survives; the single-domain photon speed does not") is more striking and more accurate about where the physics now stands.

### M4. Both theorems are applications of classical results — cite the antecedents, then claim the sharper novelty that is actually yours

Theorem 3.2 (Gram matrix of pairwise cross products = adjugate of the Gram matrix, via adj(M)ᵀadj(M) = adj(MMᵀ)) is the standard compound-matrix / Cauchy–Binet fact: the second compound of a 3×3 matrix is its adjugate up to transposition (Horn & Johnson, *Matrix Analysis*, §0.8/compound matrices). Theorem 3.4 is the classical statement that an **impedance-matched** anisotropic medium is birefringence-free: μ⁻¹ = γ det(ε) ε⁻¹ ⇔ μ ∝ ε, the condition (with ε = μ) that transformation optics *imposes by design* precisely so that both polarizations follow one effective metric (Plebanski 1960; Pendry–Schurig–Smith 2006; Leonhardt 2006). I verified both statements independently — they are true; the issue is purely attribution, and "Theorem" without antecedents invites a hostile referee's cheapest shot.

The genuinely novel and striking content is the *conjunction*: **the plaquette geometry of any three-hop lattice automatically lands on the impedance-matched point.** Discrete three-hop electromagnetism *cannot* be birefringent — no tuning, any hop vectors. Stated that way it is a better result than "our lattice's birefringence cancels." Two corollaries deserve a sentence each: (a) the null therefore does not discriminate the A=1 hop set from any other three-hop set — Sec. 4.1's discrimination claim (against birefringent rivals) is correct, but a reader should be told the null is hop-set-universal within this class; (b) the same universality is why the null survives every deferred normalization question — worth one line connecting it to the γ-independence already noted.

### M5. Auditability gap: the decisive dynamical-route evidence lives outside the paper

The "strong provisional" falsification verdict for the dynamical model (Sec. 4.4) rests on "three independent routes," of which the Ward-safe band sum — with its subtleties about the flat axial band, the diamagnetic subtraction, and the orbital-susceptibility assembly (Sec. 3.6) — is the load-bearing one. It lives in `notes/gauge_common_mode_order.md` in the repository, not in the paper; the reader cannot audit the paper's strongest dynamical claim from the paper. For a paper whose signature discipline is that every claim's evidence is tabulated, this is the one place the standard visibly slips. Bring at least the key formula, the wrong-tensor cautionary result (uniaxial about (1,−1,0) — a lovely and instructive failure), and the ~50% traceless-part number into an appendix. Relatedly: Appendices A and B are TODO stubs and the Table 1 caption promises status semantics "in the front-matter" that the front matter does not define. By the paper's own standard these are v1.0 blockers.

### M6. The A=1 normalization is a nonlinear, non-unitary modification of QM — the constraint surface is not only Lorentz tests

Sec. 2.3 is admirably careful that the per-tick renormalization is a *global nonlinear* rescale, not a unitary. But norm-restoring nonlinear evolutions are independently and severely constrained: they are of the postselection type, and by Gisin/Polchinski-style arguments generically enable superluminal signaling between entangled parties (Weinberg 1989; Gisin 1990; Polchinski 1991). Concretely: prepare an entangled state whose A-side branches differ in |k_⊥|; universal differential damping plus *global* renormalization changes B's marginals depending on A's local basis choice — a local CPTP map cannot do that, a renormalized non-trace-preserving map can. The filtering rate of M2 sets the naive scale of the effect. And note the internal tension: if the renormalization were harmless gauge, the filtering would be unobservable — but exp_01 and Sec. 2.3 insist the filtering *is* observable. The two claims need to be squared. Possible resolutions, each with different phenomenology: (a) implement A=1 as a trace-preserving CPTP map by dilation — the lost norm goes to an explicit sink sector (this dovetails with the program's four-layer "nothingness" ontology: anisotropic *absorption* into residue rather than renormalized filtering — no signaling, but now a decay channel with its own bounds); (b) a δp_min floor that cuts the differential damping off; (c) an argument that the A=1 rule, restricted to the physically preparable state space, never generates the dangerous entangled configurations. If Paper I already addresses this, cite chapter and verse here; if not, the series needs a dedicated remark, because this touches every channel that leans on the renormalization.

## 6. Program-level connections (beyond the referee role)

Three of the majors funnel into one question the program already owns: *what is the map from lattice tokens to physical matter?* M1 (flat band), M2 (filtering), and M6 (renormalization) are all statements about the bare spinor; all three soften or transform if lab particles are emergent composites (sessions) with their own dispersion — but then Sec. 4.3's observability story needs that map, and the honest v0.1 statement is "channel defined; lab connection open." Meanwhile the δp_min investigation (already a Paper III prerequisite) gains a fourth motivation: it is the natural regulator for M2's filtering divergence and one of the three outs for M6. And the four-orientation vacuum of M3 is the "hidden coexisting lattices" thread wearing different clothes — the decoherence-fingerprint framing there (structured anisotropy, recoherence) is probably the right language for whether a symmetrized vacuum can stay symmetrized in the presence of axis-bearing matter.

None of this touches the paper's verified core: the closed-form kinematics, the adjugate closure, the doubled root, and the O_h-averaging analysis are all correct as stated. The paper's problem is the opposite of the usual one — not overclaiming results, but under-claiming consequences. The same geometry that it mines so carefully for the photon sector also, on its face, freezes matter along the axis, filters amplitudes at rates far above bounds, and requires a nonlinear QM. Saying so, in the audit table, in this paper's own voice, would make it the strongest entry in the series.
