\label{sec:introduction}

A crystal is optically anisotropic when its lattice singles out an axis. The A=1
discrete causal lattice of Paper~I~\cite{menendez2026geometryfirst} singles out an
axis too, but the ``crystal'' is spacetime itself: its bipartite hop set---the three
RGB vectors $\mathbf{V}_1=(1,1,1)$, $\mathbf{V}_2=(1,-1,-1)$,
$\mathbf{V}_3=(-1,1,-1)$ and their negatives---uses three of the four cube
body-diagonals and leaves the fourth, $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_* = (1,1,-1)/\sqrt3$, out.
That omission breaks the cubic point group $O_h$ to the trigonal $D_{3d}$ about
$\hat{\mathbf{n}}_*$ and fixes an optical axis geometrically, with no new postulate.
This paper works the resulting anisotropy to closed form in the two places it
appears---the matter propagator and the vacuum's gauge response---which share the
one axis. The result is a clean survivor beside a clean no-go: the gauge-sector
birefringence cancellation is \emph{universal}---it holds for any three-hop lattice and
survives every deferred detail---while the lattice's \emph{single-domain} construction
(one fixed trigonal hop set) is a Lorentz violation in \emph{both} sectors, excluded as
constructed and awaiting a more symmetric substrate.

The motivation is falsifiability, and the word ``birefringence'' in the title is
easy to misread. The gauge-sector birefringence \emph{cancels}
(Sec.~\ref{sec:gauge_sector}), and this is sometimes taken as the framework saying
nothing. It is the opposite: a theory is falsifiable precisely when it \emph{forbids}
an observable outcome, and this framework forbids optical-axis vacuum birefringence---a
directly observable, tightly bounded quantity. Its non-observation is therefore a
stringent existing constraint the framework \emph{survives}---a falsification test
passed (as a non-trivial postdiction, since the bounds predate the framework), and one
that discriminates, since the CPT-odd, dimension-five and other birefringent
Lorentz-violating rivals instead \emph{predict} birefringence and are excluded by the
same data (Sec.~\ref{sec:falsifiability}). What that null then
does is point the forward-testable content away from birefringence and toward
\emph{dispersion}, which---because the massless and massive sectors behave
differently (Sec.~\ref{subsec:a1_normalization})---splits into channels of honestly
different status: a massive dispersion that is Planck-suppressed and sidereally
distinctive \emph{as substrate kinematics}, but connected to a laboratory particle only
through an emergence map not yet exhibited; an $\omega=0$ amplitude anisotropy that,
read as literal matter, is \emph{excluded as constructed}; and the gauge photon speed,
likewise excluded as a single domain. Section~\ref{sec:falsifiability} maps them. Being
clear about which prediction the data have already tested, which are excluded as the
bare construction stands, and which remain to be sharpened, is the point of the
exercise.

The two effects are physically different, and we name them differently. The
\emph{kinematic} anisotropy (Sec.~\ref{sec:kinematic_channel}) follows from Paper~I's
single-tick spinor transfer operator: its entire wavevector dependence passes through
one scalar structure factor whose magnitude is flat along $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_*$ and
curved perpendicular to it. This is a uniaxial \emph{directional dispersion
anisotropy}---a contrast between propagation \emph{directions}, maximal across the
axis and vanishing along it---rather than a polarization split at a fixed direction,
which is what ``birefringence'' denotes; we reserve that word for the gauge sector.
Because the transfer operator is non-unitary away from the axis, we are careful to
separate what its eigenphase magnitude establishes (an exact axial--transverse
anisotropy of the structure factor) from the further physical reading of it as a
propagating-mode group velocity, which we state as requiring the A=1 normalization
rather than assuming it. Taken at face value the same operator says more than a small
effect: the axial band is \emph{exactly} flat (a wavepacket along $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_*$
does not propagate), the kinetic anisotropy is order-unity rather than
Planck-suppressed, and an amplitude filter accumulates linearly in the lattice
spacing---so the bare single-tick spinor, read as a laboratory particle, is excluded as
constructed, with the program's token$\to$matter emergence map the open route to
viability (Sec.~\ref{subsec:as_constructed}).

The \emph{gauge} anisotropy (Sec.~\ref{sec:gauge_sector}) is the vacuum's response to
a background electromagnetic field, and here the outcome is the opposite of a new
signal. The lattice supplies two geometric plaquette quadratic forms---an electric
permittivity $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ from the on-site temporal coupling and a
magnetic inverse permeability $\boldsymbol{\mu}^{-1}$ from the spatial Peierls
holonomy---and they obey the exact algebraic identity $\boldsymbol{\mu}^{-1} =
\operatorname{adj}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon})$. This closure makes the two transverse
photon polarizations share a single dispersion, so the gauge-sector vacuum
\emph{birefringence cancels}; what remains is a direction-dependent common-mode speed
affecting both polarizations equally, which for the lattice's actual single-domain
construction is an $O(1)$ effect excluded by isotropy experiments---a genuine tension we
state plainly, and whose only escape is a more symmetric (four-orientation) construction
of the hop set (Sec.~\ref{sec:falsifiability}). The adjugate theorem and its
doubled-root consequence are the paper's central algebraic result. We are equally explicit about the load-bearing caveat: the two forms are the
natural \emph{geometric} plaquette candidates for the response blocks, and
establishing them as the full \emph{dynamical} induced action---the second-order
effective action obtained by integrating out the matter---is a step we do not
complete, so the cancellation theorem is rigorous conditional on the proposed blocks.

This gauge-sector material was developed first as a separate companion study; it is
merged here because the shared optical axis is the natural unity of the two effects.
The gauge blocks are additionally read off the running lattice engine and the null
verdict re-derived through a separate code path, which we describe as an independent
code-path verification---a useful cross-check within the same framework, engine, and
geometric objects---rather than an independent physical measurement.

\subsection{Scope}
\label{subsec:scope}

Derived here and settled: the closed-form kinematic directional dispersion anisotropy
(Sec.~\ref{sec:kinematic_channel}); the adjugate constitutive closure and the null
gauge-sector birefringence \emph{conditional on the geometric blocks}
(Sec.~\ref{sec:gauge_sector}); and the structural axis-sharing of the two effects.
Explicitly deferred: the derivation of the gauge blocks as the actual dynamical
induced action (a matter-integrated effective action; the single-object
$\operatorname{Tr}\ln T$ tensor extraction is a stated open item); the relative
electric/magnetic normalization $\gamma$ that fixes the absolute vacuum speed (a
common coupling cancels and does not set it); and the \emph{structural} resolution of
the common-mode speed anisotropy---which, for the single-domain construction, is an
$O(1)$ effect excluded by isotropy experiments (its magnitude is settled), so what
remains open is only the four-orientation / $O_h$-restoring construction that would
remove it (Sec.~\ref{sec:falsifiability}). Symmetrically for matter: the bare
single-tick spinor read as a literal laboratory particle is excluded as constructed
(the flat band, $O(1)$ kinetic anisotropy, and linearly-accumulating filter of
Sec.~\ref{subsec:as_constructed} are settled in magnitude via
\texttt{exp\_05\_matter\_sector\_order}); what remains open there is the
token$\to$matter emergence map that would connect the substrate kinematics to an
observable particle.
