On the A=1 Discrete Causal Lattice the magnetic field enters the tick rule as a
spatial link phase and yields, through the bipartite-plaquette Wilson loop, an exact
induced-action tensor with eigenvalues $\{4,4,16\}$ and optical axis $(1,1,-1)$
(Paper~I, Appendix~B). The electric field enters differently---as an on-site,
mass-like phase advance---so it admits no Wilson-loop analogue, and the corresponding
electric action block (the permittivity $\varepsilon$) is absent from both Paper~I and
Paper~II. Because the framework establishes only the spatial point group $O_h$ and not
the Lorentz boosts, there is no symmetry route from the magnetic $\mu^{-1}$ to the
electric $\varepsilon$: the electric block must be derived directly. We show that the on-site advance is
itself a temporal link, so a temporal plaquette closes and yields the permittivity
$\varepsilon=\sum_a V_a V_a^{\!\top}$ with eigenvalues $\{1,4,4\}$---the exact mirror of
the magnetic block, with the same optical axis now \emph{suppressed} rather than
enhanced---a result we read directly off the engine's tick evolution. The two blocks are
bound by an exact identity: the magnetic tensor is the \emph{adjugate} of the electric
one, $\mu^{-1}=\operatorname{adj}(\varepsilon)$, so the impedance product
$\varepsilon_i\,\mu^{-1}_i=\det\varepsilon$ is isotropic in every principal direction.
The photon dispersion then has a doubled transverse root, so gauge-sector vacuum
birefringence about $(1,1,-1)$ \emph{cancels}---a cancellation independent of the
undetermined coupling normalizations and of the specific lattice geometry. The framework
thus points to the \emph{absence} of gauge-sector birefringence, the verdict left open by
Paper~IV. This verdict is held partial pending two stated open items: a residual
common-mode anisotropy of the shared photon speed (distinct from birefringence, and
requiring its own isotropy-restoration argument), and Paper~IV's full $E{+}B$ photon
dispersion classification at large $N$. The magnetic $\{4,4,16\}$ result is retained
throughout as an exact consistency anchor.
