The optical axis $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_* = (1,1,-1)/\sqrt3$, fixed geometrically as the
cube body-diagonal the hop set omits, organizes two anisotropies. In the matter
sector it produces a closed-form uniaxial \emph{directional dispersion anisotropy},
with $M_\text{eff} = \tfrac43 P_\perp$ and a massive-sector group speed
$\tfrac23\sin\omega\,|\mathbf{k}|\sin\alpha$; the A=1 normalization earns the
group-velocity reading for $\omega\neq0$, and shows the massless eigenphase to be
exactly flat (Prop.~\ref{prop:massless_flat}). In the gauge sector, the two geometric
plaquette forms obey the adjugate relation $\boldsymbol{\mu}^{-1} =
\gamma\operatorname{adj}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon})$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:adjugate}),
which forces a doubled transverse root (Theorem~\ref{thm:doubled_root}): the vacuum
birefringence cancels.

On the paper's motivating question---what could falsify the DCL along this axis---the
verdict is deliberately sharp. Optical-axis \emph{birefringence is not a falsifier}:
it cancels, so it is a consistency null, and a frontier polarimetry bound consistent
with zero neither supports nor refutes the framework. The candidate falsifiable
content is the directional dispersion, but the same analysis that earns it as physics
also bounds its reach: it is a massive-sector effect, the massless limit does not
disperse, and so the object an astrophysical \emph{photon} test would constrain is not
the $\omega\to0$ spinor mode but the induced gauge photon, whose dispersion is the
common-mode speed anisotropy. Turning that into a frontier test therefore routes
through two open items: the observational status of the common-mode anisotropy (the
single-domain-versus-$O_h$-restoration question) and the derivation of the gauge
blocks as the actual dynamical effective action rather than geometric candidates.
Those, together with the photon-identity argument for the massless mode, are the
natural next steps---and they, not the birefringence null, are where the framework's
falsifiability will be decided. The affirmed algebra---the adjugate theorem and its
doubled-root closure---stands independently of how they resolve.
